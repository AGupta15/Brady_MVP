Brady's Game: The NFL MVP


Tom Brady lead the Patriots to the Super Bowl this season, but did he deserve the 2017 NFL Most Valuable Player award?


by Abhi Gupta and Natasha Armbrust


April 24, 2018




Every NFL regular season, teams fight through a 16 game slate which pits them against many rivals across the league. The length of the season provides a large sample to truly distinguish the bad, good, and great teams. Yet it is always difficult to recognize who is most responsible for their team’s success. The NFL anoints a “Most Valuable Player” at the end of each season to honor the player who is perceived to have contributed the most to his team. In 2017, the New England Patriots finished with a league best 13-3 record, and their Quarterback, Tom Brady, was awarded the MVP honor. This award is voted on by members of the media, who may be providing a relatively arbitrary selection that can be easily biased by personal opinion, group thinking and player exposure due to the market they play in (consider New York vs Kansas City). Throughout the season, many players emerged as candidates for the MVP when their teams entered hot streaks, and thus, we pose the question: Did Tom Brady deserve the 2017 NFL MVP?

To answer this question, we aim to statistically break down a set of quarterbacks who we consider MVP candidates in the 2017 NFL Season, and gauge their value on these metrics. It is important to note that in our methodology, we selected metrics for analysis *before* looking at the dataset, and thus aim to reduce any biases we may exhibit. Furthermore, we make this analysis available to *you* the reader, such that you can perform your own analysis. In each section, we will explain our methodology, including how we processed the data. For those who are not as familiar with football but are fans of numerical arguments, we will provide notes like *this* to supplement your knowledge of football as you read this analysis.

MVP Candidates


We picked our set of NFL MVP candidates to only include Quarterbacks. This is because QBs inherently have more value in today’s NFL as passing has taken over offensive play calling. Quarterbacks touch the ball on every play, and make the most important decisions that influence his team’s success (audibles, checking the defense and modifying the play, getting the ball from the center to the running back or receiver). Furthermore, 22.5 of the last 30 MVP Awards have gone to QBs (0.5 being attributed to Co-MVPs). Those who were fans of Todd Gurley of the LA Rams may contest this statement, but in order to complete a fair assessment of Brady, we will compare him against players who have metrics relevant to Brady, which will only include QBs. Our NFL MVP candidate set includes 12 QBs who were selected by either 1) Being initially selected to the Pro Bowl or 2) Leading a pass heavy offense (for more than 8 games played). This includes every QB who received votes for the MVP, and thus covers a wide swath of players. We compare (in no particular order):

1.Tom Brady, NE
2. Carson Wentz, PHI
3. Roethlisberger, PIT
4. Philip Rivers, LAC
5. Russell Wilson, SEA
6. Drew Brees, NO
7. Matt Ryan, ATL
8. Case Keenum, MIN
9. Cam Newton, CAR
10. Jared Goff, LAR
11. Alex Smith, KC
12. Matt Stafford, DET

Metrics of Analysis


We will compare Brady to each of the other 11 QBs and gauge his value on 3 metrics:

1.Effectiveness in Passing the Ball Downfield
This dimension analyzes QB accuracy by looking at completion percentage. We look at effectiveness by pass distance, accuracy by down, and accuracy by point spread in the game. Each of these graphs highlight the QB’s effectiveness based on pass difficulty (distance), short-term difficulty (down), and long-term difficulty and pressure (point spread).

2.Effectiveness by Drive Efficiency
This metric looks at the efficiency of a QB based on a series of metrics including time per possession, yards per drive, points per drive, and plays per drive. This evaluates a QB on the quality of the drives they play in by looking at speed of scoring, length, and rate of progress.

3.Win Probability Impact
We look for player value by looking at his impact on his team’s win percentage probability, utilizing a slope graph. By comparing offense and defense win probability, the slope from defense to offense will indicate how much that given player impacts their team.

Methodology


To compute our analysis metrics, we rely on the nflscrapR-data dataset, which contains a play by play documentation across the 2017 NFL Season. The author originally scraped all the data from the NFL API and joined many datasets together to form this one.

Quarterbacks who appear in our visualizations are narrowed by the number of pass attempts they have across the 2017 NFL Season. We include a QB if they have attempted at least 100 passes, as this provides a reasonable body of work at around 4 games of playing time. Any quarterback who has not attempted this many passes is not included in our graphs. This narrowed our set of Valid Passers to 45 QBs, who can be seen across every visualization.
